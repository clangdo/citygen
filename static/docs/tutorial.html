Getting Started

Is cityscripting necessary?

In short, no. The first thing to know about cityscript is that generating cities works without any scripting! A blank script will provide you with a full set of default values for everything the service needs to generate something that looks decent (subjectively).

Further, if you'd like to tweak just one thing, you don't have to override every default, you can selectively edit the appearance of the city.

In order to observe the results of the default settings, you can click the big green generate button in the interface without typing a word into the script. You can open the interface in a new tab and close it when you're finished to return here.

Shortly after clicking, you should see a city image pop up in an "Albedo" panel on the right side of the editor. This is because you sent a blank cityscript to the service, so it used fully default settings for everything.

Making our city smaller

One of the simplest things to do is change the dimensions of your city. There are two sets of dimensions that cityscripting can change, the city dimensions, and the image dimensions. Maybe we want to have a much smaller area appear, but we want to keep the number of pixels in the image the same. We can do this by changing the city dimensions. By default we have a 1km x 1km city, you can find this, and other defaults in the reference.

To shrink our rendered city we can type the following. 

let city.width be 500m

let city.height be 500m

It's recommended to use units for distances, but it's not imperative, cityscript understands m for meters and km for kilometers.

Now that you've typed that (make sure each let statement is on its own line), go ahead and click the Generate button again, you should see much larger rectangular buildings now, and the streets should look larger too!

Distributions, distributions, distributions

You may notice that if you click the generate button multiple times you won't be left with the same city, this is because the roads and gaps between buildings are randomly placed based on a density distribution.

Basically, gaps in the city are spaced out with a random number. And you can control the range of this random number.

For example, maybe you want a city where the blocks are tall rectangles. To get that kind of look, the horizontal density of the streets would have to be higher than the vertical density.

The city streets have a two dimensional density distribution. To make them taller than they are wide we can do the following

For example, maybe you want a city where the blocks are tall rectangles. To get that kind of look, the horizontal density of the streets would have to be higher than the vertical density. To do this, you can do the following. 

let roads.density.x.max be 20/km
let roads.density.x.min be 10/km
let roads.density.y.max be 5/km
let roads.density.y.max be 3/km

You can also change the distribution type. Most things are normally distributed by default, but some are uniformly distributed, if we wanted our streets to be spaced with a uniform distribution rather than a normal distribution, we could use the following 

let roads.density.x.distribution be uniform
let roads.density.y.distribution be uniform

Distributions can also be constant, in which case they'll always use the max value. Distributions can also be skewed (when they're normal), like shown below, make sure to delete your uniform changes, otherwise the skewing below won't do anything. 

let roads.density.x.skew be 2.0
let roads.density.y.skew be 0.5

See how this can also be a good way to get a rectangular look?

Conclusion

Now you're ready to mess with some things, try changing the following distributions. 
roads.density.x
roads.density.y
roads.breadth
buildings.density.x
buildings.density.y
buildings.roof.tint
buildings.roof.border
sidewalks.breadth
alleys.breadth

buildings.walls.stepback

That last one steps the back walls of buildings further from the street, too far and you may get overlapping. Anyway, enjoy playing!
